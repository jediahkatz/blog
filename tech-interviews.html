The ideology of tech interviews

Every fall, recruiting season manifests as a massive source of stress for preprofessional students in tech-adjacent fields. At Penn, this is most of my peers — recruiting exists in our collective consciousness as an always-hovering authoritarian parent and a scapegoat for all sorts of ills (real quote: “I can’t even think about dating until recruiting is over”).

But while interviewing is usually a source of stress for anyone, what is it that prompts some students to spend hours each day preparing, for weeks or even for months? The answer lies in the particular format of interviews for engineering positions at modern tech companies. The dreaded coding interview begins with the interviewer presenting the candidate with a challenging problem meant to be solved by a computer program. The rest of the interview is steered by the candidate, who must figure out on the spot how to write such a program.

Typically, the candidate can’t come up with a full answer right away. Instead, interviewer and interviewee engage in an elaborate partner dance: the candidate chanting their thought process out loud, exploring promising ideas, and abandoning unsuccessful ones, while prodding the interviewer to offer affirmation, catch missteps, and bestow precious hints. The clock casts a long shadow — a single tough problem can take upwards of half an hour to solve, and failing to do so before the interview’s conclusion heralds a poor outlook.

The specifics of this type of interview, however, matter much less than the surprisingly radical philosophy that underlies it. The coding interview throws traditionally key concerns — experience, personality, breadth of knowledge — on the back burner in favor of raw problem-solving prowess.

What does this mean, exactly? Problem-solving deals with abstraction, not specifics. The company cares much less about the problem and the eventual solution than it does about the steps taken to get there. In a document released to interview candidates, Google warns:

Our engineers are evaluating not only your technical abilities but also how you approach problems and how you try to solve them. … We want to understand how you think.

lmplicit in this focus on problem-solving is an ideology that many students and professionals in tech have internalized without realizing, and one that is worth examining closely. Essentially, the claim is that experience does not really matter in the long term, only reasoning ability. The underlying logic says that a sufficiently intelligent and rational thinker doesn’t need preexisting knowledge, because anything they need to know can be “learned on the fly.”

Before unpacking this, let’s look at a bit of history. The philosophy of the modern coding interview was inherited from another type of interview: the case interview, which was created by the management consulting industry and is still the primary format for consulting interviews. The case interview is very similar to the coding interview, but instead of a computational problem for the interviewee to solve, there is a business scenario. It’s hard to find information about the origins of the case interview, but as far as I can tell, the oldest case competition (ICBC) was first held in 1978, and the first book about the case interview (the Wharton MBA Case Interview Study Guide) was published in 1997. So it seems to have been developed over the latter half of the 20th century.

This link reveals that the ideology of the coding interview is closely linked to the ideology of management consulting. It makes perfect sense from this point of view — consultants typically have little knowledge in advance about their specific client companies, but they try to apply abstract knowledge about the industry or even just business in general. The tech industry has embraced this way of thinking with gusto — not just internally in its hiring practices (hire smart people and spend months onboarding them), but also externally in the way that new ventures and products are conceived. There’s a whole crop of companies, from Airbnb to Redfin to Flexport, that seek to “disrupt” existing industries, utilizing lots of technology but relatively little industry experience.

The ideology of techno-optimists and management consultants is, on its face, radically meritocratic. The notion that we should not glorify experience — that no field is immune to criticism and disruption — destabilizes traditional forces of hierarchy and perhaps even disrupts the institutionalization of bad practice and narrow perspective that is all too common in the corporate world.

But this ideology is also arrogant. Assuming that members of the professional intelligentsia can find their way in any scenario is a narrow perspective of its own and trivializes forms of intelligence that don’t translate well to the whiteboard. And although the tech world likes to think of itself as the largest meritocracy in the world, this myth has been repeatedly debunked. But the myth itself is harmful, because it legitimizes incorrect decisions and can be wielded with blunt force against valid criticism.

Ultimately, I have faith in the many tech workers who are aware of these issues and recognize that not every problem can be reasoned out from first principles. But when an interview purports to evaluate “how you think,” it’s always worth asking what the “right” way to think really is.
